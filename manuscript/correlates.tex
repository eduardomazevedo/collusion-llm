We now examine the correlates of collusive communication. We consider three types of correlates: firm sector, firm size, and call year. We use LLM-flagged as our binary indicator of collusive communication. This has the issue of being a noisy proxy for collusive communication, due to the significant rate of false positives. But the other measures are also imperfect, and LLM-flagged has the advantage of a larger sample size.

The clearest pattern is the heterogeneity by sector, displayed in Figure \ref{fig:segment_tag_rates}. We split the sample into top level GICS sector definitions. There is considerable heterogeneity around the average rate of collusive communications of \data{correlates_collusive_communication/llm_tagged_collusive_pct_percentage1}. The sector with the lowest rate of collusive communications is health care (\data{correlates_collusive_communication/sector_healthcare_rate_percentage1}), and the highest is materials (\data{correlates_collusive_communication/sector_materials_rate_percentage1}). This is intuitive, as materials includes segments with undifferentiated commodities and high returns to scale.

We also measured the rate of collusive communications in a few segments that are notorious for common collusion. Almost all of them have significantly higher rates of collusive communications than the average. The highest rates are paper \& packaging and cement \& glass, with rates about eight times the average. The only exception is heavy construction, which interestingly has a much lower rate of collusive communications than the average. A possible reason is that this segment is known for collusion with bidding rings in public works projects. But this type of collusion is not facilitated by public communications as in the other high collusion segments.
