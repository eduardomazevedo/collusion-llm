In industries such as airlines, steel, and truck rentals, firms have coordinated to raise prices using public announcements such as earnings calls. 
This paper studies collusive content in a large corpus of firms' public communications. 
A large language model (LLM) is used to examine almost 500,000 company transcripts. The LLM is selective in that only \data{summary_stats/tagging_performance/llm_validation_rate_overall_sample_pct_percentage2} of the transcripts 
satisfy the criterion for collusive content. A human audit of the \data{summary_stats/tagging_performance/human_audit_sample_count_int} transcripts with the highest LLM scores identifies systematic ways in which the communication is facilitating coordinated conduct. 
The audit evaluates the performance of the 
LLM in substituting for human experts and thus assesses its suitability as a screening tool for competition agencies and plaintiff law firms.
