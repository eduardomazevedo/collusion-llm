We now examine the correlates of collusive communication. We consider three types of correlates: firm sector, firm size, and call year. These covariates are not available for all firms, so the sample sizes for each of these analyses differ somewhat. There are \data{correlates_collusive_communication/sector_valid_observations_int} firms in the sector analysis, \data{correlates_collusive_communication/mkvalt_valid_observations_int} firms in the size analysis, and \data{correlates_collusive_communication/year_valid_observations_int} firms in the year analysis.

We have three main measures of collusive communication:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Whether a transcript is in the LLM-flagged set.
    \item Whether a transcript is in the LLM-flagged set and also validated as collusive by the follow-up LLM queries.
    \item Whether a transcript is in the LLM-flagged set, validated as collusive by the follow-up LLM queries, and also confirmed as collusive by human audit.
\end{enumerate}

The main issue with using these measures is that they are imperfect. They reflect a mix of the likelihood of collusive communication and the likelihood of LLM detection regardless of whether the communication is collusive. The first measure is the broadest, so it has the advantage of greater statistical resolution but at the cost of lower reliability. Fortunately, it turns out that the main patterns are similar across the LLM-flagged and LLM-validated measures. This partially assuages concerns about the reliability of the measures. Unfortunately, the human audit measure is too small to draw statistically meaningful conclusions. Given this, we focus on the LLM-flagged set and report results for the other two measures in the supplemental material.

Figures \ref{fig:sector_tag_rates}, \ref{fig:market_value_deciles}, and \ref{fig:year_tag_rates} show the fraction of communications tagged as collusive by the LLM across different industry sectors, market value deciles, and years, respectively.

The clearest pattern is the heterogeneity by sector. Materials is an outlier, with more than triple the average probability of collusive communication. This is followed by energy, with over twice the average probability. Consumer staples, communications and services, and industrials are also significantly above average. We highlight that these correlational results are tentative, due to the imperfect nature of our LLM measures and the high rate of false positives. Nevertheless, the fact that the patterns are similar across the LLM-flagged and LLM-validated measures suggests that the results may indeed represent heterogeneity in the prevalence of collusive communication across sectors.

The patterns by market value decile are less clear. The largest firms, except in the top decile, have a slightly higher than average probability of collusive communication. While the differences are statistically significant, they are not large, and it is unclear whether these are due to actual differences in collusive communication or simply due to unrelated differences in the likelihood of LLM detection.

Likewise, Figure \ref{fig:year_tag_rates} suggests a mild decrease in collusive communication over time. While we see statistically significant differences across these years, these are relatively weak and noisy patterns.
