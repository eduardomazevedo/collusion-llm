\subsection{Data Sources}

Our primary data source consists of transcripts from public corporate communications obtained through S\&P Capital IQ. This dataset encompasses all available public calls, not solely quarterly earnings calls. Coverage includes earnings calls, investor day presentations, industry conferences, analyst meetings, and other public corporate communications.

The transcripts span from \data{summary_stats/dataset_overview/first_date} to \data{summary_stats/dataset_overview/last_date}, covering \data{summary_stats/dataset_overview/unique_companies_int} companies across diverse industries and sectors. Our dataset comprises \data{summary_stats/dataset_overview/total_transcripts_int} transcripts in total. The average call lasts \data{summary_stats/audio_characteristics/mean_length_description}. In total, it would take \data{summary_stats/audio_characteristics/total_audio_years_float} years of uninterrupted audio to listen to all calls. Capital IQ provides verbatim transcriptions of these calls, including both the prepared remarks by company executives and the subsequent question-and-answer sessions with analysts and investors. This broader scope beyond traditional earnings calls is particularly valuable for detecting potential collusive behavior, as discussions of competitive dynamics and capacity decisions may occur in various corporate communication settings.

Each transcript in our dataset includes metadata such as the company identifier, call date, call type, participating executives, and the full text of the discussion. The inclusion of diverse call types strengthens our analysis by capturing a wider range of contexts in which executives might inadvertently signal anti-competitive intentions or discuss market coordination.

\subsection{Extracting Data from Transcripts}

Our approach leverages Large Language Models to systematically identify potential collusive intent in corporate communications at scale. The core challenge lies in replicating the nuanced judgment of human experts who can detect subtle signals of anti-competitive coordination. While human reviewers possess the expertise to identify collusive language in earnings calls, manually reviewing nearly half a million transcripts would be prohibitively resource-intensive. Conversely, automated analysis through LLMs offers computational efficiency but requires carefully crafted prompts that capture the complexity of collusive signaling while producing reliable, interpretable outputs.

We employ zero-shot prompting, where the LLM analyzes each transcript without explicit input-output examples. Initial prompt iterations revealed the importance of specification clarity. A basic prompt such as ``Does the following transcript show evidence of potential collusive intent?'' proved inadequate for several reasons: it fails to define collusive intent operationally, does not specify how such intent manifests in corporate communications, and lacks guidance on output structure (binary classification, continuous scale, or extracted evidence).

Through iterative refinement, we developed prompts that incorporate insights from the industrial organization literature on tacit collusion. The literature emphasizes that firms may signal coordination through discussions of capacity discipline, pricing intentions, and conditional statements linking their actions to competitor behavior. We examined outputs from a small set of transcripts to identify which prompt elements effectively captured these nuances while maintaining consistency across diverse industry contexts.

Following established prompting best practices from both LLM providers and the research community, we structured our prompts to request three key outputs: a collusion severity score ranging from 0 to 100, providing a continuous measure of intent strength; a concise reasoning statement explaining the score assignment; and specific excerpts from the transcript supporting the assessment. This multi-faceted output enables both quantitative analysis and qualitative validation of the LLM's determinations.

Prompt refinement proceeded through systematic benchmarking against human expert assessments. We utilized two validation datasets. The first are conference call transcripts from U-Haul, some of which include collusive communications and are discussed in \textcite{harrington2022collusion}. We reviewed the transcripts and provided collusion severity scores on a 0-100 scale. The other validation dataset consists of airline industry transcripts flagged by \textcite{aryal2022coordinated} for collusive content. We used their publicly available binary assessment of collusion. To make these two validation sets comparable, we also produced a binary classification on the U-Haul sample using a threshold of 75. Qualitative review of discrepancies between LLM and human assessments informed further prompt adjustments, particularly in cases where the model either over-detected competitive language as collusive or missed subtle coordination signals.

Our final prompt instructs:
\begin{quote}
You’re an expert in detecting subtle collusive intent in company earnings calls. You know firms can try to avoid scrutiny if they express intentions to reduce supply or increase prices, or they can imply it would be good if industry capacity were limited or prices were kept high. Analyze the following transcript of a public company communication and identify any signs that the company is trying to coordinate with competitors to limit capacity or keep prices high. Provide a score from 0 to 100, where 0 means no evidence of collusive intent and 100 means strong evidence of collusive intent. Additionally, provide a very brief explanation of your assessment. Also provide a list of excerpts that support your assessment.
\end{quote}

With the prompt finalized, we processed the complete corpus of transcripts through OpenAI's API using their batch processing infrastructure. This approach enabled efficient parallel processing while respecting API rate limits. The processing took several days, but could run continuously in the background without manual intervention. Once the processing finished, we had the LLM response strings stored in a large database, structured such that we could easily clean them up and extract the score, reasoning and set of excerpts for analysis.

Throughout the process of refining the prompt, we use LLM versions as released by OpenAI (primarily 
\textit{gpt-4o-mini}), so we do not train a model specifically for the task at hand. However, OpenAI do provide options to request and pay for a fine-tuned version of one of their models, either based on a sample of input-output pairs, or on a set of more/less preferred output versions for every given input provided in the training sample. The goal of choosing eithe option is to get the general-purpose LLM to produce output that is hopefully more relevant and precise to a specific goal, but is not guaranteed to do so. Moreover, OpenAI recognize in their related documentation that for data extraction tasks, extensive prompt engineering is the first step to pursue, and only upon seeing no improvement does it become potentially helpful to opt for fine-tuning services. They also suggest that users should expect only modest performance improvements from fine-tuning.
